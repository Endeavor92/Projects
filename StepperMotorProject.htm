Since Ernő Rubik first invented the Rubik�s Cube in 1974, people from all around the world have competed to see who can solve it the fastest. By the 21st century, and machines, with the help of computer algorithms have become contenders in this field. The NEMA 17 Stepper Motor is a perfect tool in this project considering its precision, having to make 90 degree turns and high speeds all while providing enough torque to spin a Rubik�s Cube. The machine requires input of an array of type String as a set of instruction for stepper motors. Hopefully in the next iteration, it would be a closed system such that no human interaction would be required to solve the cube.�

The mechanism was planned to have six NEMA 17 stepper motors, one for each face of the cube, connected then to a A4988 Stepper Motors Driver. The step and direction input on the drivers are controlled by the Arduino. The Arduino code passes the algorithm to solve the Rubik�s cube, via a String array and then converts into instructions for the stepper motors through its PWM pins connected to the stepper motor drivers. The stepper motor drivers� logic supply voltage is powered by the Arduino and has a maximum rating of 5.5 V. The maximum rating of its supply voltage is 35 V. The supply voltage is powered by a 19 V/1.58 A DC power adapter that was taken from a laptop charger with re-soldered ends.�

One of the most crucial parts of the set-up was to limit the motor current on the A4988 driver. The 19 V DC source is higher than what the NEMA 17 is rated for, so to ensure that the motor is does not burn out. In Figure 3, a voltmeter is applied to the potentiometer and ground where the potentiometer can be turned to the correct amount given by the equation V = IMAX * 8 * RSEN where IMAX is the maximum current rating of the NEMA 17 stepper motor and RSEN is the A4988 driver�s current sensing resistance, commonly 0.068 Ω. The 19 V DC supply was connected to a 100 μF capacitor (shown in Figure 1), to offset any voltage spikes. Note that one must be careful when working with the circuit and must turn off/disconnect any power source.�

Figure 3: Voltmeter reading of potentiometer and ground. The potentiometer must be turned accordingly to the current limit of the NEMA 17 Stepper Motor.�

��������������� I had originally bought a total of 11 A4988 drivers in the case that I fried one. Me being reckless, I accidently removed the stepper motors from the driver, while the power was on and fried 6/11 of the drivers, leaving me with only 5 left. To confirm this, PWM signals on a working driver are sent to the motor via its 4 output pins. On a working driver, pulses up to ~2 V are sent to the motor, but on checking the fried drivers, no voltages were being sent to the motor. The remaining 5 A4988 drivers were replaced leaving me to manually turn one of the faces of the Rubik�s Cube. If the algorithm read in a move on a non-working side (non-working meaning that no stepper motor is attached) it would give an extra delay of 6 seconds. This would give me ample time to recognize the turn and move the Cube accordingly.�

����������� The actual project is incomplete considering that I fried 6/11 A4988 drivers. But moving forward, to improve on the design would be to create a closed system that would not require any human input for the machine to solve the Rubik�s Cube. More specifically, to implement camera(s) that would read current state of the cube, and have the cube solve it directly. This would require an expanded Arduino such as the Arduino Mega 2560. From the video, you can also see that I had to readjust the cube almost every single time it made a move. This is due to the Velcro straps connecting the stepper motor to the faces of the cube. Since the connection between the two parts was not rigid / sticky enough, often it did not end with a clean rotation. Recurring less-than-perfect rotations would cause the Rubik�s cube to unattach from the robot at the Velcro straps. The next iteration would include a more rigid connection between the Rubik�s cube and the motors.�
